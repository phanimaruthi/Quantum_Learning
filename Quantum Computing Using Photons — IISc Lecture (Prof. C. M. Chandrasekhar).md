## üå± Understanding Quantum Computing

One of the key takeaways was the clear distinction between **classical computing** and **quantum computing**:

- Classical systems use bits (`0` or `1`)
- Quantum systems use **qubits**, which can exist in multiple states at once

The lecture reinforced my understanding of two fundamental principles:

- **Superposition** ‚Äì enables a qubit to represent multiple states simultaneously  
- **Entanglement** ‚Äì creates strong correlations between qubits that cannot be explained classically  

These principles explain why quantum computers can outperform classical systems for specific problems.

---

## üí° Why Photons Are Used as Qubits

The lecture explained why photons are a strong candidate for implementing qubits. I learned that photons:

- Are **less affected by environmental noise**, helping preserve quantum states
- Can often operate without extreme cooling requirements
- Are naturally suited for **quantum communication and networking**
- Can be precisely controlled using mature optical technologies

This helped me understand why photonic systems are widely studied for scalable and distributed quantum computing.

---

## üîß Encoding Quantum Information in Photons

I learned that quantum information can be encoded in photons using different physical properties, such as:

- **Polarization states**
- **Spatial paths (path encoding)**
- **Time bins or frequency modes**

Each encoding method allows photons to represent qubit states and enables different ways of implementing quantum gates using optical components.

---

## üß† How Computation Is Performed

The lecture showed how **optical elements** act as quantum gates in photonic systems. These include:

- Beam splitters  
- Mirrors  
- Waveplates  
- Phase shifters  

I also understood the difference between:

- **Probabilistic operations**, where gate success depends on measurement outcomes  
- **Deterministic operations**, which are necessary for building scalable quantum processors  

This highlighted a major challenge in photonic quantum computing: achieving reliable, repeatable gate operations.
